Configurational Forces

Abstract

With a loose analogy to penetration problems in biomechanics (e.g., penetration through a cell's membrane or a cell being suctioned by a pipette), we investigate the role of configurational forces during the process of inserting a compliant intruder into an elastic structure. When inserting into a rigid constraint, we find a configurational force proportional to the square of the strain required to deform the penetrating body. This force has a more complex structure when considering the compliance of the constraint and, in all cases, it tends to expel the intruding body.

Galerkin Method for Stepped Structures

Abstract

In these studies, we investigate the free vibrations of stepped structures and present two versions of the Galerkin method. Firstly, we apply the straightforward Galerkin method, which involves conducting the Galerkin procedure in each subdomain or step of the structure. Secondly, we introduce the rigorous realization of the Galerkin method, in which structural parameters like rigidity and mass are treated as generalized functions across the entire domain. This advanced approach employs unit step functions, the Dirac's delta function, and its derivative to account for changes in structural parameters between the steps. Importantly, the rigorous implementation introduces additional terms that are not present in the straightforward or 'naïve' version of the Galerkin method. Both versions of the Galerkin method are compared to exact solutions for the analyzed problems. As the number of terms in the expansion increases, the rigorous Galerkin method, which is based on generalized functions, converges towards the exact solution. In contrast, the commonly used 'naïve' realization of the Galerkin method, prevalent in the literature, does not exhibit this convergence. These studies underscore the need for careful consideration when implementing the Galerkin method for stepped structures, emphasizing the importance of employing the rigorous version for more accurate results.

KISADAMA

Abstract

The objective of the KISADAMA project is to develop an integrated approach for modeling and analyzing the decay mechanisms of masonry structures, specifically those made from fired clay or sandstone bricks, caused by salt crystallization. The idea is to combine theoretical, numerical, and experimental studies at the (sub)micro-scale to model the interaction between salt crystallization and deformation/damage of the porous masonry material. This information will then be extrapolated to the macro-scale to create effective predictive tools that are valuable from an engineering perspective. This approach aims to achieve a comprehensive understanding of crystallization processes and the ability to predict future damage scenarios.

Related papers:

  • Gentilini, C., D'Altri, A. M., Amato, M., Zanotti, P., Favaro, F., & de Miranda, S. (2017). Salt attack effects on the shear behavior of masonry: preliminary results of an experimental campaign. In Key Engineering Materials (Vol. 747, pp. 512-517). Trans Tech Publications Ltd.
